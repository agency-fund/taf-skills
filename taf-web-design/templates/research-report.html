What we learned funding family caregivers in maternal health

A twelve-month evaluation across three TAF-backed organizations, asking whether structured counseling shifts behavior at the household level.

Summary

One paragraph

Counseling adherence rose 34%, postnatal visits rose 21%, and behavior change persisted at the 12-month follow-up — across all three sites, with stronger effects where the household primary caregiver (not just the patient) received training.

Background

What we set out to measure

Ample research demonstrates that well-tailored counseling and coaching can enable people to unlock remarkable outcomes. Less is known about who in the household needs to receive the counseling for change to take root. This evaluation asked that question across three TAF-backed organizations.

Each organization was already running a counseling program. We co-designed an evaluation that measured both the patient-level adherence and the household-level practices — recognition of warning signs, escalation behavior, postnatal visit attendance.

Method

How we ran it

  • Semi-structured interviews with 42 frontline counselors
  • Pre/post survey instrument across three sites
  • Administrative data on visit attendance and clinical outcomes
  • Co-analysis workshop with each grantee’s research team

Sample sizes ranged from 380 to 720 households per site. The full methodology, including the survey instrument, lives in the supplementary materials.

Findings

Three things changed

Counseling adherence rose 34%

Households with a trained family caregiver completed a full counseling sequence at a substantially higher rate than the control group. The effect was largest in households where the counselor was a mother-in-law or older sibling.1

Postnatal visits up 21%

Trained caregivers reported faster recognition of warning signs, leading to earlier postnatal visits across all three sites. This was the strongest single behavioral indicator and the one most likely to persist.

Sustained at 12 months

Behavior change persisted at the 12-month follow-up, suggesting durable household-level practice change — not just a Hawthorne-style response to being studied.2

When a mother-in-law learns the warning signs, the whole household watches differently. We didn’t just train one person — we changed who the household trusts. Field counselor · Bihar, India
Discussion

Why this matters for funders

The household, not the patient, is the unit of behavior change in maternal health. This shifts how we evaluate counseling programs and where we direct capital. Programs that train a single point of contact per household — typically the patient — leave the most consequential decision-makers untrained.

This finding is not novel in the academic literature, but it is novel in the way TAF-backed organizations now operationalize counseling. Two of the three grantees have already adjusted their training curricula in response.

Recommendation

Where we’re directing the next round

Double down on family-caregiver training as a unit of investment, not just the patient or the counselor. The data is clear; the model travels. We expect to commit an additional $4M across this cohort over the next two years.
