Case Study · Maternal Health · India

A frontline counselor can change a family’s trajectory.

How Noora Health is training family caregivers — and what we learned funding them through three program cycles.

The grantee

Noora Health

Noora Health trains family caregivers — the mothers, sisters, mothers-in-law, husbands — who are present at the moment a discharge happens. The premise is direct: train the person who carries the patient home, not just the patient.

The Agency Fund has backed Noora across three program cycles, with $1.2M in unrestricted grant capital and infrastructure support. This page summarizes what we learned.

The story

What changed

Before the training, household members watched the patient. After the training, they watched the warning signs. A small shift in language, a large shift in behavior.

When a mother-in-law learns the warning signs, the whole household watches differently. We didn’t just train one person — we changed who the household trusts. Field counselor · Bihar, India

What we funded

Three program cycles. Each cycle: train ~50,000 caregivers across two states, measure behavior change at 3 / 6 / 12 months, refine the curriculum, hand the next cohort the better version.

What surprised us

The senior household member — usually a mother-in-law or older sister — was the most consequential trainee. Programs that defaulted to training the patient missed the actual decision-maker.

Where it travels

The model is portable: any clinical setting that discharges patients to family care can use it. Two of our other grantees have begun adapting elements of the Noora curriculum for their own contexts.
